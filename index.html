Deal Overview

Keurig Dr Pepper Inc. (NASDAQ: KDP) announced on March 27 that its tender offer for all outstanding shares of JDE Peet's N.V. (EURONEXT: JDEP) has been declared unconditional. During the offer period, 96.22% of JDE Peet's shares were tendered, representing approximately EUR 14.9 billion in aggregate value. Settlement is scheduled for April 1, 2026. A post-closing acceptance period runs through April 13.

KDP is paying EUR 31.85 ($37.26) per share in cash, a 33% premium to JDE Peet's 90-day volume-weighted average price. The total deal is valued at approximately $18.4 billion, financed through roughly $9 billion in long-term debt, $8.5 billion in equity capital, and $5 billion in assumed JDE Peet's bonds.

The plan after closing is extraordinary: KDP intends to split into two independent, publicly traded companies. "Global Coffee Co." will combine KDP's Keurig and Green Mountain brands with JDE Peet's portfolio (L'OR, Jacobs, Douwe Egberts, Peet's Coffee), creating a $16 billion annual revenue coffee business in 100+ countries. "Beverage Co." will house Dr Pepper, 7Up, Canada Dry, Snapple, and Ghost, with over $11 billion in annual sales. CEO Tim Cofer will lead Beverage Co. from Frisco, Texas. CFO Sudhanshu Priyadarshi will run Global Coffee Co. from Burlington, Mass. and Amsterdam.

Why It Matters

Buy to break: This is one of the largest "acquire to separate" transactions in consumer goods history. KDP is spending $18 billion not to become a bigger conglomerate, but to build a coffee company large enough to justify standalone scale, then immediately spin it off. The logic: two focused businesses will each command higher valuations than one bloated one.

The Nestle problem: Global Coffee Co. would be the world's largest pure-play coffee company, directly challenging Nestle's Nespresso and Nescafe franchises with a multi-format portfolio spanning single-serve pods, whole bean, instant, and out-of-home. Nestle had the global coffee market largely to itself. It doesn't anymore.

JAB's endgame: JAB Holding Company, the investment vehicle of the Reimann family, engineered JDE Peet's through years of coffee acquisitions (Jacobs Douwe Egberts, Peet's Coffee, Stumptown). The KDP deal lets JAB monetize at a premium while their portfolio gets absorbed into a larger platform with better distribution.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Execution on separation: Running a combined entity while simultaneously planning a split into two public companies is operationally brutal. Any delay or misstep in the separation (targeted by late 2026) would drag on both businesses.

Leverage load: $9 billion in new debt plus $5 billion in assumed bonds. KDP's struggling U.S. coffee business (declining K-Cup volumes) now carries the financing burden for a premium-priced acquisition.

Brand portfolio overlap: Integrating dozens of regional coffee brands across 100 countries while maintaining quality and distribution is a multi-year exercise with real attrition risk.

✓ Bull Case

$400M synergy target: Cost synergies over three years from overlapping supply chains, procurement, and corporate overhead. Management projects roughly 10% EPS accretion in year one.

Geographic unlock: KDP is dominant in North America but has negligible international coffee presence. JDE Peet's is strong in Europe, Latin America, and Asia-Pacific. The combined footprint fills each other's gaps almost perfectly.

Beverage Co. gets cleaner: Separating coffee lets the soda and energy business (Dr Pepper, Ghost, Canada Dry) tell a simpler growth story to investors, likely commanding a higher standalone multiple.

Deal Overview

Novartis AG (SIX: NOVN) announced on March 27 that it has agreed to acquire Excellergy Inc., a private Palo Alto-based biotech developing next-generation anti-IgE therapies for allergic diseases, for up to $2 billion in upfront and milestone payments. The transaction is expected to close in the second half of 2026.

The acquisition brings Exl-111, a half-life extended, high-affinity anti-IgE antibody currently in Phase 1 clinical trials. Unlike existing anti-IgE therapies such as Novartis's own Xolair (which blocks free IgE before it binds), Exl-111 is designed to target IgE molecules already bound to immune cells, potentially enabling faster and deeper suppression of allergic responses.

This is Novartis's second multi-billion-dollar acquisition in seven days. On March 20, the company agreed to acquire Pikavation Therapeutics from Synnovation for up to $3 billion to secure a next-generation breast cancer drug. Combined with the $12 billion Avidity Biosciences deal (closed February 2026), the $1.5 billion SciNeuro Alzheimer's deal (January), and the $1.7 billion Unnatural Products cardiology deal (February), Novartis has committed over $20 billion in acquisitions since the start of the year.

Why It Matters

The patent cliff is real: CEO Vas Narasimhan has acknowledged that Novartis is absorbing $4 billion in patent expirations this year alone. Xolair's U.S. formulation patent expired in November 2025, and global sales are forecast to decline from roughly $3 billion at peak to $1 billion by 2031. Excellergy's Exl-111 is the replacement pipeline.

The allergy franchise rebuild: Xolair was co-developed with Roche's Genentech and became the first FDA-approved treatment for food allergies in February 2024. Exl-111's trifunctional mechanism of action (targeting IgE already bound to effector cells) represents a potential generational leap. If confirmed clinically, the drug could address food allergy, chronic urticaria, allergic asthma, and pediatric populations.

Acquisition velocity as strategy: Novartis is not doing one transformative deal. It is doing six or seven mid-sized deals simultaneously across immunology, oncology, neuroscience, and cardiology. The portfolio approach spreads clinical risk but requires flawless integration across every therapeutic area.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Phase 1 risk: $2 billion for a Phase 1 asset is aggressive. Exl-111 has preclinical data and early pharmacokinetic results, but no efficacy readout yet. The drug is years from market.

Competitive field: GSK recently acquired RAPT Therapeutics for $2.2 billion with a food allergy treatment further along in development.

Integration bandwidth: Novartis is now integrating Avidity, Pikavation, SciNeuro, Unnatural Products, and Excellergy simultaneously.

✓ Bull Case

Known biology: Novartis has 20+ years of IgE expertise from Xolair. This is not a new therapeutic area; it is an upgrade to an existing franchise with established commercial infrastructure.

Massive TAM: Food allergy alone affects 17 million Americans. Chronic urticaria, allergic asthma, and other IgE-mediated conditions expand the addressable population significantly.

Deal Overview

Henkel AG (XETRA: HEN3), the German consumer goods conglomerate behind Persil, Schwarzkopf, and Dial, announced on March 26 that it has agreed to acquire Olaplex Holdings Inc. (NASDAQ: OLPX) for $2.06 per share in an all-cash transaction, valuing the company at approximately $1.4 billion. The deal was unanimously approved by Olaplex's board, and controlling shareholder Advent International has committed to vote in favor.

The offer price represents a 55% premium to Olaplex's March 25 closing price of $1.33 per share. Olaplex generated approximately $423 million in FY2025 revenue with adjusted EBITDA of roughly $94 million (22% margin), down substantially from its peak profitability.

For context: Olaplex went public in September 2021 at $21 per share, reaching a market capitalization above $13 billion. It is being acquired for roughly one-tenth of that valuation.

Why It Matters

The cautionary IPO tale: Olaplex was the prestige hair care brand that was supposed to prove you could build a venture-scale outcome in beauty. Then came a hair loss lawsuit, increased competition, and slowing growth. The stock went from $25 at IPO to $1.33. Henkel's $2.06 offer is a mercy exit.

Henkel's strategy is working: CEO Carsten Knobel merged Henkel's laundry and beauty divisions into a single "Consumer Brands" unit and has been acquiring aggressively. Olaplex gives Henkel a premium, science-led brand with strong professional salon relationships and DTC e-commerce presence.

Advent's painful math: The $1.4 billion takeout is a fraction of Olaplex's peak valuation. For PE sponsors who rode the 2021 IPO wave, this is a case study in what happens when the market reprices growth.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Brand perception damage: A 2023 lawsuit alleging hair loss dented Olaplex's reputation. Rebuilding consumer trust under new ownership takes years.

Declining margins: Adjusted EBITDA margin fell from 38% to 22% in two years. Henkel needs to stabilize profitability before it can extract value.

Specialty retail weakness: Olaplex's specialty retail channel declined 14.5% in Q4 2025, even as professional and DTC channels grew.

✓ Bull Case

Distribution unlock: Henkel operates in 80+ countries. Olaplex is primarily North American. The international expansion opportunity is real and immediate.

The science still works: Olaplex's bond-building chemistry remains differentiated. Under Henkel's R&D infrastructure, innovation can accelerate without public-market pressure.

Deal Overview

Merck & Co. (NYSE: MRK) announced on March 25 that it has entered into a definitive agreement to acquire Terns Pharmaceuticals Inc. (NASDAQ: TERN), a clinical-stage oncology company, for $53.00 per share in cash, representing an equity value of approximately $6.7 billion ($5.7 billion net of acquired cash). The offer represents a 31% premium to the 60-day and 42% premium to the 90-day volume-weighted average stock price.

The crown jewel is TERN-701, an oral allosteric BCR::ABL1 tyrosine kinase inhibitor currently in a Phase 1/2 trial (CARDINAL) for Philadelphia chromosome-positive chronic phase CML. The FDA granted Orphan Drug Designation for TERN-701 in March 2024. This is Merck's third multi-billion-dollar acquisition in the past year.

Why It Matters

The Keytruda countdown: Keytruda generated over $25 billion in 2024 revenue and is the world's best-selling drug. Its U.S. patent expires in 2028. Every deal Merck makes right now is an attempt to replace even a fraction of that revenue. TERN-701 is positioned as a potential best-in-class therapy for CML, a market where Novartis's Scemblix currently leads.

The speculation tax: Terns' stock rose roughly 6x from its January lows as investors bet the company would be acquired. By the time Merck showed up, the premium to Tuesday's close was only 6%. The real premium was already baked in. This is what happens when the market collectively identifies a target before the acquirer pulls the trigger.

Hematology as a beachhead: Merck's existing hematology pipeline includes candidates in Phase 3 for leukemias, lymphomas, and myeloproliferative neoplasms. TERN-701 slots into organizational infrastructure that is already built.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Early-stage premium: $6.7 billion for a Phase 1/2 asset with no pivotal data. If TERN-701 fails in later trials, the $5.8 billion write-off will be one of pharma's most expensive.

Scemblix entrenchment: Novartis's Scemblix is already approved and generating revenue in CML. TERN-701 needs to demonstrate superiority to gain meaningful share.

Keytruda cliff math: No combination of pipeline drugs will fully replace $25B+ in annual Keytruda revenue. Investors will scrutinize whether Merck's deal spree is building real value or delaying the inevitable.

✓ Bull Case

Best-in-class potential: TERN-701's allosteric mechanism targets BCR::ABL1 differently than existing TKIs, with early data suggesting activity against resistant mutations.

Infrastructure fit: Merck's existing hematology teams mean TERN-701 doesn't need a standalone organization. Integration costs should be modest relative to deal size.

At roughly 9.5x NTM revenue before synergies (closer to 5.5x after), TI is betting it can make the economics work through manufacturing leverage no other acquirer could offer.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Regulatory timeline: Closing in H1 2027 means 15+ months of antitrust review. Semiconductor deals face elevated scrutiny in the current policy environment.

Fab migration risk: Moving production to TI's fabs requires re-qualifying every product with every customer. Yield or quality issues during transition could lose design wins.

IoT cycle sensitivity: Smart home and industrial IoT are cyclical. The previous year saw sharp inventory corrections across the wireless chip market.

✓ Bull Case

Manufacturing moat: TI's 300mm fabs are among the most cost-efficient in the analog/mixed-signal world. No fabless competitor can replicate the per-unit cost advantage.

Matter protocol tailwind: The Matter smart home standard is driving consolidation in wireless protocols. Silicon Labs is a leading Matter chip supplier with design wins across major OEMs.
