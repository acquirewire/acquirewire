Deal Overview

Cintas Corporation (Nasdaq: CTAS) and UniFirst Corporation (NYSE: UNF) announced on March 11, 2026, that they have entered into a definitive agreement under which Cintas will acquire UniFirst for $310.00 per share — $155.00 in cash plus 0.7720 shares of Cintas stock for each UniFirst share, based on Cintas' closing price of $200.77 on March 9. The implied enterprise value is approximately $5.5 billion, representing 8.0x run-rate trailing twelve months EBITDA including projected synergies.

Cintas identified approximately $375 million in annualized operating cost synergies, expected to be fully realized within four years of close. Financing comes from cash on hand, committed lines of credit, and a fully committed bridge from Morgan Stanley, KeyBank, and Wells Fargo. Pro forma leverage at close is expected at approximately 1.5x debt-to-EBITDA — lean enough to preserve Cintas' investment grade rating. The transaction was unanimously approved by both boards and is backed by a voting support agreement from the Croatti family, which controls roughly two-thirds of UniFirst's voting power. Cintas has agreed to a $350 million reverse termination fee if regulators block the deal.

This is Cintas' third run at UniFirst. A 2022 approach was rebuffed. A $5.3 billion bid in early 2025 was withdrawn in March of that year after UniFirst's board declined to engage. The March 2026 agreement — at a higher price, with the Croatti family finally on board — ends one of the longest corporate courtships in industrial services history.

Why It Matters

A near-duopoly in workwear: The combined entity will command an estimated 45–50% of the North American uniform rental and facility services market. The only remaining national competitor is Vestis, the former Aramark uniform business. In dozens of mid-sized regional markets where Vestis lacks a processing plant, the merger effectively creates a local monopoly. That's the FTC's problem to solve — and Cintas knows it, hence the $350 million reverse termination fee.

The "Cintas-ization" thesis: Cintas has done this before. Its 2017 acquisition of G&K Services validated a simple operational blueprint: absorb a lower-margin competitor's route density, apply Cintas' superior processing efficiency and pricing discipline, watch EBITDA margins expand. UniFirst's margins have consistently trailed Cintas by several hundred basis points. The $375 million synergy figure is built on that spread.

Route density is the real prize: Uniform rental is a logistics business masquerading as a services business. What Cintas is buying is UniFirst's route network — the trucks, plants, and customer relationships that would take a decade to replicate organically. With greater density per truck, Cintas drives down cost-per-stop across the combined footprint. That's where the synergies actually come from, not headcount cuts.

⚠ Risks to Watch

FTC scrutiny is the deal's existential risk: A combined 45–50% market share in a route-based service industry with high switching costs is exactly the structure regulators target. The FTC is expected to require divestitures in specific local markets — the question is how many and whether they impair the synergy case.

Labor monopsony argument: Cintas and UniFirst are the two largest employers of route drivers and laundry technicians in many regions. The 2026 regulatory framework weighs workforce effects alongside consumer harm, giving the DOJ a second avenue to challenge or condition the deal.

Culture and ERP integration: Cintas runs a hyper-competitive, metrics-driven culture. UniFirst has operated as a family-controlled, relationship-first business for decades and spent years mid-cycle on its own ERP transformation. Merging two different back-office systems without customer attrition requires execution Cintas hasn't attempted at this scale since G&K.

✓ Bull Case

Proven playbook: The G&K acquisition in 2017 is the template. Cintas absorbed a major rival, hit its synergy targets, and expanded margins. Management has described having "muscle memory" for exactly this integration — the current deal team ran G&K.

Margin expansion math is compelling: UniFirst's EBITDA margins have consistently run 4–6 percentage points below Cintas'. Applying Cintas-level margins to the UniFirst revenue base — even partially — generates hundreds of millions in incremental earnings well beyond the $375 million synergy guide.

Healthcare tailwind: An aging U.S. population is driving sustained demand for healthcare laundry and protective garment services — a segment where UniFirst has meaningful exposure and Cintas wants to grow. The combined entity becomes the dominant supplier to hospitals and clinics at the right moment in the demographic cycle.

Deal Overview

CACI International Inc. (NYSE: CACI) completed its $2.6 billion all-cash acquisition of ARKA Group L.P. on March 9, 2026. The seller was Blackstone Tactical Opportunities, which first backed ARKA in 2019 and spent the next five years bolt-on assembling it into a full-spectrum space intelligence platform — acquiring Amergint, Tethers Unlimited, Danbury Mission Technologies, Stratagem Group, Lumacron, and finally the radar and sensing business from Maxar in 2024.

The deal was announced December 19, 2025 and cleared regulatory approvals in under 80 days. CACI financed the acquisition entirely with cash and expects a tax benefit with a present value of approximately $225 million embedded in the transaction structure. ARKA is projected to contribute roughly $650 million in revenue and $150 million in EBITDA over the next twelve months, implying purchase multiples of approximately 4.0x NTM sales and 18x NTM EBITDA. More than 1,100 ARKA employees — 315 of them software engineers — joined CACI at close.

ARKA's core asset is its Danbury, Connecticut facility, which manufactures electro-optical/infrared (EO/IR) and hyperspectral imaging sensors for satellites. The company also operates ground-based software processing and agentic AI systems that convert raw sensor data into actionable geospatial intelligence for the DoD and Intelligence Community. Roughly half of ARKA's revenue mix is classified.

Why It Matters

The Golden Dome angle: CACI was explicit in its investor presentation that ARKA's capabilities are positioned directly for the Golden Dome missile defense initiative — the administration's marquee defense modernization program. Space-based sensors that can track hypersonic threats in real time are not a commodity. ARKA has the Danbury manufacturing depth and the cleared customer relationships to be a primary supplier. You can't build that in three years.

The vertical integration play: CACI has historically been an IT services and signals intelligence firm — the "pipes" side of national security. ARKA gives it the sensors and the AI software to own the full kill chain from collection to analysis. That's a fundamentally higher-margin, stickier business than managing government IT contracts.

Blackstone's exit: Blackstone assembled ARKA through six acquisitions over five years — a classic platform build — and sold at a time when defense multiples are elevated and space intelligence spending is accelerating. At ~18x EBITDA on a business that didn't exist as a single entity in 2019, they extracted maximum cycle value.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Classified revenue opacity: Half of ARKA's revenue is non-disclosed. CACI investors are underwriting a business they can't fully see, at a premium multiple, in a program environment that can shift with a single budget cycle.

Integration of six bolt-ons: Blackstone stitched ARKA together from disparate acquisitions. Whether those pieces share technology, culture, and processes — or merely a holding company — becomes CACI's problem at close.

Budget risk: CACI funded with cash but consumed a significant portion of its balance sheet. A continuing resolution or CR-extended budget environment would pressure ARKA's new contract wins at exactly the wrong time.

✓ Bull Case

Irreplaceable hardware: The Danbury EO/IR manufacturing facility took decades to build. There is no shortcut to replicating its production capability for the classified satellite programs it serves.

Multi-domain intelligence moat: CACI's existing signals intelligence combined with ARKA's space-based sensing creates a multi-source intelligence platform that competes directly with much larger primes — at a fraction of their overhead.

Agentic AI in the right place: ARKA's agentic AI software is purpose-built for classified environments — exactly the use case the DoD is prioritizing under its February 2026 directive on sovereign AI for tactical edge missions.

Deal Overview

Nexstar Media Group (Nasdaq: NXST) closed its $6.2 billion acquisition of TEGNA Inc. on March 19, 2026, making TEGNA a wholly owned subsidiary. The deal, announced August 19, 2025, priced each TEGNA share at $22.00 in cash — a 31% premium to the unaffected 30-day average ending August 8, 2025. Financing came via a $2.39 billion senior first-lien bridge facility and a new $2.75 billion term loan B, alongside existing Nexstar liquidity.

The combined entity controls more than 265 full-power television stations across 44 states and Washington D.C., operating in 132 of the nation's 210 TV markets. Combined 2025 pro forma revenue is approximately $7.66 billion. As a condition of FCC approval, Nexstar committed to divest six stations within two years, expand local news investment in acquired markets, and freeze retransmission rates for existing pay-TV distributor agreements through November 30, 2026.

The critical unlock was the FCC's decision — under Chairman Brendan Carr — to waive the decades-old 39% national television household ownership cap. That rule had effectively blocked broadcast consolidation at this scale for years. Without the waiver, the deal was structurally impossible.

Why It Matters

The retransmission consent weapon: Local TV stations get paid by cable and satellite providers to carry their signals. The bigger your footprint, the harder it is for a distributor to drop you — which means higher per-subscriber fees at renewal. Nexstar now operates in 132 markets. DirecTV, which sued to block this deal, knows exactly what that leverage looks like in a negotiation. The lawsuit is not altruistic.

A political deal as much as a financial one: President Trump endorsed the merger on Truth Social in February. FCC Chairman Carr signed off within weeks. The framing — consolidation as a bulwark against "Big Tech" and coastal media — gave the administration a narrative reason to wave it through. The eight state AGs who sued the day before closing (California, New York, Colorado, Illinois, Oregon, North Carolina, Connecticut, Virginia) see it differently.

Precedent for the rest of the industry: If this holds, broadcast consolidation accelerates. Sinclair, Gray Television, and others are watching whether the state AG suits succeed in unwinding the deal or die in court. The answer shapes every subsequent broadcast M&A conversation in 2026.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Active litigation: Eight state attorneys general filed suit the day before closing and immediately sought a temporary restraining order. DirecTV also sued on antitrust grounds. Even a partial injunction could force divestitures or rate concessions beyond what the FCC already required.

Structural decline in linear TV: Cord-cutting continues to erode the base that retransmission consent fees depend on. Nexstar just levered up to buy scale in a medium whose audience shrinks roughly 8% annually. The synergies need to materialize fast.

Debt load: Nexstar took on over $5 billion in new facilities to fund this. The combined pro forma showed a net loss of $171 million for 2025 on a fully consolidated basis, driven by interest expense. A political ad market downturn in a non-election year would stress the model.

✓ Bull Case

Retransmission pricing power: Operating in 80% of U.S. TV households gives Nexstar the leverage to reset distributor contracts at materially higher rates. Even a modest per-subscriber fee increase across the TEGNA footprint moves the needle significantly.

2026 is a midterm setup year: Political ad spending cycles every two years. With a midterm election in November 2026, Nexstar's expanded local news footprint becomes a premium political advertising vehicle at exactly the right moment post-close.

Cost structure rationalization: Two overlapping station groups with separate back-office infrastructure, technology stacks, and sales organizations. The synergy opportunity in G&A and technology consolidation is real even if revenue synergies take time.

Deal Overview

Ecolab Inc. (NYSE: ECL), the $60B water and hygiene services company, announced on March 20 that it has entered into a definitive agreement to acquire CoolIT Systems in an all-cash transaction valued at $4.75 billion. The seller is a consortium of KKR and Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth fund Mubadala Investment, who jointly acquired CoolIT in June 2023 for approximately $270 million — meaning they are walking away with a roughly 17.6x return in under three years.

CoolIT is a Calgary-based manufacturer of direct liquid cooling (DLC) systems for hyperscale and colocation data centers. The company designs and builds coolant distribution units (CDUs), cold plates, and direct-to-chip cooling technologies. CoolIT is projected to generate approximately $550 million in revenue over the next twelve months, implying a purchase multiple of roughly 8.6x NTM sales and 29x forward EBITDA.

The acquisition doubles Ecolab's stated total addressable market in its Global High-Tech segment from $5 billion to $10 billion.

Why It Matters

The physical layer thesis: The AI infrastructure buildout has officially reached the pipes, fluids, and thermal management systems that determine whether a $30,000 GPU cluster actually stays online. Ecolab already serves more than 1,000 data centers. What it was missing was the actual cooling hardware. CoolIT fills that gap completely.

The installed base play: Ecolab was willing to pay 29x EBITDA because CoolIT's value isn't just current revenue — it's the long-term service contracts that attach to every CDU installation. Liquid cooling loops require ongoing chemical management. Every piece of CoolIT hardware installed is a future Ecolab chemistry customer.

KKR's exit: One of the best PE outcomes of the decade. They bought CoolIT in mid-2023 for $270 million. Three years later they're selling for $4.75 billion — a 17.6x return that validates the "AI picks and shovels" investment thesis.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Leverage at 3x: Ecolab is financing entirely with debt. A slowdown in hyperscaler capex would pressure both CoolIT's growth and Ecolab's ability to deleverage on schedule.

Technology obsolescence: Direct-to-chip cooling could be displaced by immersion cooling over a 5–7 year horizon. At 29x EBITDA, there's zero margin of safety if CoolIT's technology loses share.

Integration complexity: CoolIT is a Calgary hardware company. Ecolab is a Minnesota services giant. Blending those cultures without losing the product team is non-trivial.

✓ Bull Case

Unbeatable bundle: No competitor can sell both the cooling hardware and the chemical treatment to keep it running. This cross-sell flywheel is a genuine moat.

Secular trend: The shift from air to liquid cooling is structural, driven by GPU power density. CoolIT has 25 years of depth and relationships with the largest hyperscalers.

Deal Overview

Berkshire Hathaway Inc. (NYSE: BRK.B) announced that its reinsurance subsidiary National Indemnity Company (NICO) has acquired a 2.49% strategic equity stake in Tokio Marine Holdings Inc. — Japan's largest P&C insurer — for roughly $1.8 billion. The transaction includes a 10-year strategic partnership covering reinsurance collaboration and joint M&A activity.

The structure is notable: Tokio Marine will use the proceeds to repurchase its own shares, neutralizing dilution. NICO joins Tokio Marine's reinsurance panel in a whole account quota share — a live operational arrangement, not a passive investment.

This marks the first major international initiative under Greg Abel, who assumed the CEO role from Warren Buffett on January 1, 2026.

Why It Matters

A different playbook: Buffett's Japan strategy was passive — buy trading houses, collect dividends, wait. Abel is doing something operationally intensive. A quota share ties NICO's results directly to Tokio Marine's portfolio. The two companies need to actually work together on risk appetite and pricing discipline. This is an operational thesis, not a financial one.

The M&A pipeline: Tokio Marine targets $10 billion in international acquisitions through 2027. Berkshire has $160B+ in cash. Together they create a credible vehicle for acquiring specialty insurance carriers across ASEAN and North America.

For Tokio Marine: A reinsurance backstop from the most financially secure insurer on the planet, plus the Berkshire name at a moment of international expansion.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Cultural gap: Berkshire's decentralized, returns-first culture vs. a 147-year-old Japanese institution. A major catastrophe year would stress-test alignment immediately.

Currency exposure: The $1.8B is yen-denominated. A 10% depreciation costs ~$180 million.

Abel unproven internationally: Berkshire investors expect Buffett-level discipline. A messy joint acquisition would sharpen scrutiny fast.

✓ Bull Case

Cycle timing: Reinsurance pricing at cycle highs. Entering a quota share now is the insurance equivalent of buying real estate in 2009.

$10B acquisition pipeline: Tokio Marine has the mandate and market knowledge; Berkshire has the capital. Multiple motivated sellers globally in specialty insurance.
