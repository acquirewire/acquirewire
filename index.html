Deal Overview

Merck & Co. (NYSE: MRK) announced on March 25 that it will acquire Terns Pharmaceuticals (NASDAQ: TERN), a clinical-stage oncology company, for $53.00 per share in an all-cash tender offer. The deal values Terns at approximately $6.7 billion in equity, or $5.7 billion net of acquired cash. That price represents a 6% premium to Tuesday's close, a 31% premium to the 60-day VWAP, and a 42% premium to the 90-day VWAP.

Terns' stock had already surged roughly sixfold over the prior six months on the strength of Phase 1 data for its lead asset, TERN-701, and widespread speculation that the company was an acquisition target. Both boards have approved the deal, which is expected to close in Q2 2026 pending antitrust clearance and a majority tender.

Merck expects to record an approximately $5.8 billion charge (roughly $2.35 per share) in its Q2 and full-year 2026 results related to the acquisition.

Why It Matters

The Keytruda imperative: Keytruda generated $29.5 billion in 2024 revenue and accounts for roughly 40% of Merck's pharmaceutical sales. U.S. patent protection expires in late 2028, with IRA-negotiated Medicare price cuts kicking in the same year. Merck is cutting $3 billion in costs and buying its way into replacement revenue streams. Terns is the latest, and one of the largest, of those bets.

Best-in-class clinical data: TERN-701, an oral allosteric BCR-ABL inhibitor for chronic myeloid leukemia, posted a 74% overall major molecular response rate in heavily pretreated patients in the Phase 1 CARDINAL study, with a 36% deep molecular response rate by 24 weeks. Those numbers are roughly double the rates seen in comparable Phase 1 trials of approved or in-development CML therapies. No dose-limiting toxicities were observed.

The Scemblix race: TERN-701 targets the same allosteric binding pocket as Novartis's Scemblix (asciminib), which is already approved and growing rapidly. In preclinical studies, TERN-701 showed greater potency against several resistance mutations. If Merck can move TERN-701 through pivotal trials and into first-line CML, the peak revenue opportunity is measured in billions.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Phase 1 price tag: $6.7 billion for a drug that has not yet entered pivotal trials. If TERN-701 stumbles in Phase 3, Merck takes a multi-billion dollar writedown with no revenue to show for it.

Scemblix head start: Novartis already has an approved, commercial-stage allosteric inhibitor with expanding indications. TERN-701 needs to prove superiority, not just equivalence, to capture meaningful share.

Narrow pipeline: Terns is essentially a single-asset company. There is no fallback program if TERN-701 disappoints.

✓ Bull Case

Unprecedented Phase 1 data: 74% MMR and 36% deep molecular response in a heavily pretreated, refractory population. These are the best early-stage CML numbers the field has seen.

Merck's hematology infrastructure: Merck already has Phase 3 candidates across leukemias, lymphomas, and myeloproliferative neoplasms. TERN-701 slots into an existing organizational machine, not a greenfield build.

Patent cliff math: If TERN-701 reaches even $3 to 4 billion in peak sales, it materially offsets the Keytruda revenue gap. That math justifies the premium.

Deal Overview

Thermo Fisher Scientific (NYSE: TMO) completed its acquisition of Clario Holdings on March 24 for $8.875 billion in cash at close, with an additional $125 million deferred payment due in January 2027 and up to $400 million in performance-based earnouts tied to Clario's 2026 and 2027 results. Total consideration could reach approximately $9.4 billion.

Clario was acquired from a private equity consortium led by Astorg and Nordic Capital, with Novo Holdings and Cinven also participating. Clario generated approximately $1.25 billion in 2025 revenue and employs roughly 4,000 people globally. The company will join Thermo Fisher's Laboratory Products and Biopharma Services segment.

Thermo Fisher expects the deal to be immediately accretive to adjusted operating margin, add $0.45 to adjusted EPS in year one, and generate approximately $175 million in adjusted operating income from synergies by year five. Management projects high-single-digit revenue growth for the Clario business under Thermo Fisher ownership.

Why It Matters

The data infrastructure play: Clario's endpoint data platform has been involved in roughly 70% of all novel drug approvals by the FDA and EMA over the past decade. That is not a market share number you can replicate. Thermo Fisher is buying the data backbone of clinical trials: the systems that collect, manage, and analyze clinical evidence across every phase of drug development.

Vertical integration logic: Thermo Fisher already supplies lab equipment, reagents, and contract research services to biopharma. Adding Clario's clinical data layer means the company can now serve a pharma customer from molecule discovery through regulatory submission. Every integration point is a cross-sell opportunity and a switching cost for the customer.

PE exit mechanics: The earnout structure is worth noting. Up to $400 million in contingent payments gives the selling PE consortium meaningful upside if Clario performs, while protecting Thermo Fisher's downside. It also signals that both sides believe the growth trajectory is real.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Biotech funding dependency: Clario's growth is directly tied to clinical trial volumes. A sustained biotech funding downturn would pressure the business regardless of its competitive position.

AI disruption: Endpoint data collection and analysis is exactly the kind of workflow that AI-native competitors are targeting. Thermo Fisher needs to invest heavily to keep Clario's platform ahead.

Integration with existing CRO: Thermo Fisher's PPD contract research unit and Clario must be integrated without disrupting active clinical trials. That is a complex operational challenge.

✓ Bull Case

Irreplaceable installed base: 70% of novel drug approvals over a decade. Pharma companies do not switch endpoint data providers mid-program. Retention is structural.

Revenue synergies over cost synergies: The $175M synergy target is primarily revenue-driven, not headcount reduction. That is a healthier, more sustainable synergy profile.

Double-digit IRR: Management is projecting double-digit internal rate of return with high-single-digit organic growth. At 7.1x revenue for a sticky, recurring data business, the math works if growth holds.

Deal Overview

Ecolab Inc. (NYSE: ECL), the $60B water and hygiene services company, announced on March 20 that it has entered into a definitive agreement to acquire CoolIT Systems in an all-cash transaction valued at $4.75 billion. The seller is a consortium of KKR and Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth fund Mubadala Investment, who jointly acquired CoolIT in June 2023 for approximately $270 million, meaning they are walking away with a roughly 17.6x return in under three years.

CoolIT is a Calgary-based manufacturer of direct liquid cooling (DLC) systems for hyperscale and colocation data centers. The company designs and builds coolant distribution units (CDUs), cold plates, and direct-to-chip cooling technologies. CoolIT is projected to generate approximately $550 million in revenue over the next twelve months, implying a purchase multiple of roughly 8.6x NTM sales and 29x forward EBITDA.

The acquisition doubles Ecolab's stated total addressable market in its Global High-Tech segment from $5 billion to $10 billion.

Why It Matters

The physical layer thesis: The AI infrastructure buildout has officially reached the pipes, fluids, and thermal management systems that determine whether a $30,000 GPU cluster actually stays online. Ecolab already serves more than 1,000 data centers. What it was missing was the actual cooling hardware. CoolIT fills that gap completely.

The installed base play: Ecolab was willing to pay 29x EBITDA because CoolIT's value isn't just current revenue. It's the long-term service contracts that attach to every CDU installation. Liquid cooling loops require ongoing chemical management. Every piece of CoolIT hardware installed is a future Ecolab chemistry customer.

KKR's exit: One of the best PE outcomes of the decade. They bought CoolIT in mid-2023 for $270 million. Three years later they're selling for $4.75 billion, a 17.6x return that validates the "AI picks and shovels" investment thesis.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Leverage at 3x: Ecolab is financing entirely with debt. A slowdown in hyperscaler capex would pressure both CoolIT's growth and Ecolab's ability to deleverage on schedule.

Technology obsolescence: Direct-to-chip cooling could be displaced by immersion cooling over a 5 to 7 year horizon. At 29x EBITDA, there's zero margin of safety if CoolIT's technology loses share.

Integration complexity: CoolIT is a Calgary hardware company. Ecolab is a Minnesota services giant. Blending those cultures without losing the product team is non-trivial.

✓ Bull Case

Unbeatable bundle: No competitor can sell both the cooling hardware and the chemical treatment to keep it running. This cross-sell flywheel is a genuine moat.

Secular trend: The shift from air to liquid cooling is structural, driven by GPU power density. CoolIT has 25 years of depth and relationships with the largest hyperscalers.

Deal Overview

Berkshire Hathaway Inc. (NYSE: BRK.B) announced that its reinsurance subsidiary National Indemnity Company (NICO) has acquired a 2.49% strategic equity stake in Tokio Marine Holdings Inc., Japan's largest P&C insurer, for roughly $1.8 billion. The transaction includes a 10-year strategic partnership covering reinsurance collaboration and joint M&A activity.

The structure is notable: Tokio Marine will use the proceeds to repurchase its own shares, neutralizing dilution. NICO joins Tokio Marine's reinsurance panel in a whole account quota share, a live operational arrangement, not a passive investment.

This marks the first major international initiative under Greg Abel, who assumed the CEO role from Warren Buffett on January 1, 2026.

Why It Matters

A different playbook: Buffett's Japan strategy was passive: buy trading houses, collect dividends, wait. Abel is doing something operationally intensive. A quota share ties NICO's results directly to Tokio Marine's portfolio. The two companies need to actually work together on risk appetite and pricing discipline. This is an operational thesis, not a financial one.

The M&A pipeline: Tokio Marine targets $10 billion in international acquisitions through 2027. Berkshire has $160B+ in cash. Together they create a credible vehicle for acquiring specialty insurance carriers across ASEAN and North America.

For Tokio Marine: A reinsurance backstop from the most financially secure insurer on the planet, plus the Berkshire name at a moment of international expansion.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Cultural gap: Berkshire's decentralized, returns-first culture vs. a 147-year-old Japanese institution. A major catastrophe year would stress-test alignment immediately.

Currency exposure: The $1.8B is yen-denominated. A 10% depreciation costs ~$180 million.

Abel unproven internationally: Berkshire investors expect Buffett-level discipline. A messy joint acquisition would sharpen scrutiny fast.

✓ Bull Case

Cycle timing: Reinsurance pricing at cycle highs. Entering a quota share now is the insurance equivalent of buying real estate in 2009.

$10B acquisition pipeline: Tokio Marine has the mandate and market knowledge; Berkshire has the capital. Multiple motivated sellers globally in specialty insurance.

Deal Overview

Baker Hughes Company (NASDAQ: BKR) agreed to acquire Chart Industries (NYSE: GTLS) for $210 per share in cash, a $13.6 billion enterprise value at a 22% premium. Financing comes via bridge debt from Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley. Pro forma leverage: 2.25x, targeting 1.0 to 1.5x within 24 months. Baker Hughes identified $325 million in annualized cost synergies.

Chart Industries manufactures cryogenic equipment, heat exchangers, and industrial gas handling equipment. FY2024 revenue: $4.2 billion, adjusted EBITDA: $1.0 billion. Chart's equipment is deployed in approximately 90% of LNG projects globally.

The deal displaced Flowserve's $19 billion all-stock merger of equals. Flowserve collected a $266 million termination fee.

Why It Matters

Identity transformation: Baker Hughes CEO Lorenzo Simonelli has called the company an "energy technology company" for three years. Chart makes that description accurate. Chart's markets (LNG, hydrogen, carbon capture, data center cooling) are the growth verticals Baker Hughes has been building toward.

LNG dominance: Chart's cryogenic equipment in ~90% of LNG projects is unreplicable. The combined entity can bid on entire project packages rather than individual line items.

Data center crossover: Chart's cooling equipment is being adapted for high-density data centers, the same AI infrastructure trend driving Ecolab's CoolIT deal. Baker Hughes arrives at the same destination from a different direction, at larger scale.

⚠ Risks to Watch

Complex integration: Two large manufacturers with distinct cultures. The $325M synergy target requires consolidating manufacturing and retaining specialized engineers.

LNG sensitivity: Chart's largest segment is LNG. A sustained pricing decline or renewable acceleration pressures the core business.

Leverage risk: 2.25x at close depends on both companies performing well during integration and divestitures proceeding on schedule.

✓ Bull Case

LNG moat: 90% market share in cryogenic equipment built over decades. Installed base creates predictable aftermarket revenue.

Attractive on synergies: 9x fully synergized EBITDA. Double-digit EPS accretion projected in year one.

Multi-vertical platform: LNG, hydrogen, carbon capture, and data center cooling are four independent demand drivers. Not a single-sector bet.
